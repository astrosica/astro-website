Research

Galactic magnetism ; multiphase interstellar medium ; radio polarization ; interstellar extinction ; star formation

My primary research interest lies in our Galaxy's complex multiphase interstellar medium (ISM). Currently, my PhD thesis focuses on using magnetic tracers in different phases of the ISM to improve our understanding on our Galaxy's multi-phase Galactic magnetic field. In the past, I have also worked on Galactic radio loops and bubbles, early low- and high-mass star formation, interstellar dust extinction, and astrochemistry. As a result, I have experience using observational data from a variety of radio, infrared, and optical telescopes such as ALMA, Arecibo, JCMT, DRAO, Parkes, Planck, and GBT.

Magnetic Field Coupling Between the Diffuse Warm Ionized and Cold Neutral Phases of S1-C (Campbell et al., 2020 in prep.)


Our Galaxy contains a complex arrangement of interstellar media that is magnetized and highly turbulent. Spatial gradients of the complex radio polarized sky reveal a network of filamentary structures interpreted as turbulent-driven changes in the line-of-sight magnetic field strength and/or thermal electron density (Gaensler et al., 2011) primarily in the warm ionized medium (WIM). Pictured (left) is a GALFACTS radio polarization gradient map (red) towards S1-C, a region in the high-latitude diffuse ISM, showing two prominent filaments. Also shown is 21 cm GALFA-HI data (blue) at individual velocity channels spanning a range of +/- 20 km/s. It has been shown that narrow structures in HI called HI fibers (Clark et al., 2014, 2015) are density structures residing in the cold neutral medium (CNM) and are very well coupled to the ambient magnetic field. We have found that the polarization gradient filaments are well aligned with HI fibers across this broad velocity range, indicating possible magnetic field coupling between the WIM and CNM.

On the Bright Infrared Stars (BIRS) in the W3 Giant Molecular Cloud
(Campbell et al., 2020 in prep.)

I am working on characterizing the interstellar extinction curve in the W3 giant molecular cloud with Peter Martin using an assortment of optical and infrared photometry. Dust grains in the ISM scatter and absorb background starlight, causing stars to appear more red than they actually are. By characterizing how this extinction changes across frequency, we can obtain the interstellar extinction curve. The extinction curve is not well understood even for the diffuse ISM where the gas is mostly atomic, and extinction studies of massive star forming regions where the ISM is mostly molecular is significantly lacking. It is difficult to decouple extinction from intrinsically red objects (e.g., pre-main sequence stars, red giants) so we are targeting massive OB stars whose intrinsic colours are well known (and not red). Pictured (left) is sample spectral energy distribution (SED) for a B0.5-B2V star overlaid with the CCM extinction model for the Galactic average (dashed red) and the typical molecular ISM (solid red). This particular star is well modelled above 50 microns, however, it appears to exhibit an infrared excess possibly from free-free emission.

A Magneto-ionic Radio Polarization Study of the Orion-Eridanus Superbubble,
Loop III, and the IVA


As my first introduction to Galactic magnetism, I worked under the supervision of Bryan Gaensler to characterize the magnetic field properties of Galactic-scale filaments, loops, and bubbles in our Galaxy. Such structures are some of the largest features in the Galactic sky and while they were discovered in the 60's, their origin still remains unclear. Pictured (left) is a schematic that depicts how triggers such as SNe and stellar winds can cause overdensities in the circumstellar media (grey) and compress associated magnetic field lines (red) which are seen as increased field strengths. To attempt to address the nature of these structures, I performed a multi-frequency analysis using a variety of continuum, polarization, and spectral data from surveys including the Global Magneto-ionic Medium Survey (GMIMS), Continuum HI Parkes All-Sky Survey (CHIPASS), HI 4-pi (HI4PI), and Wisconsin H-alpha Mapper (WHAM).

Contraction Signatures of Dense Cores in the Perseus Molecular Cloud
(Campbell et al., 2016)


My first undergraduate research project was supervised by Rachel Friesen and Peter Martin on low-mass star formation. Low mass stars like our Sun form in dense cores within molecular clouds, but the details of how these pre-stellar objects evolve to their stellar counterparts remains unclear. I worked on a JCMT molecular line survey of pre-stellar dense cores in the Perseus molecular cloud to search for signatures of core infall and related their infall signatures to tracers of core evolution (e.g., Jeans mass, virial mass, non-thermal linewidth). The figure (left) shows the cores' ratio of mass to Jeans mass (left) and non-thermal linewidths (right) as a function of their infall velocities. The results of this project led to my first first-authored paper as well as my first conference poster presentation, both of which can be accessed via the links below.

Constructing Python Software to Analyze Spatially Resolved ALMA
Spectroscopy of IRAS 16293-2422

During my second last year of undergraduate studies, I visited Leiden University in the Netherlands to attend the Leiden/ESA Astrophysics Program for Summer Students (LEAPS). I worked with Mihkel Kama and Magnus Persson on characterizing the astrophysical conditions of the popular chemically-rich hot corino IRAS 16293-2422 using spatially resolved, high spectral resolution data cubes obtained with the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA). While ALMA's capabilities provide significant advantages for molecular spectroscopy of chemically-rich objects, analyzing such complex high-quality data proves to be very challenging, so I wrote Python software capable of analyzing such data. The software I wrote was used to created moment maps (intensity, velocity, and velocity dispersion) fand the population diagram technique was used to determine the total column density of each of the detected molecular species. Imaged (left) is a set of such maps for one of the many detections; these are a) intensity, b) velocity, c) velocity dispersion, and d) total column density. This project greatly challenged my programming skills, and improved my knowledge of the impact that star formation has on the surrounding interstellar medium as well as the challenges that lie in high resolution spectroscopy.

Using High-Resolution JCMT Images to Complement Herschel in Regions of
Massive Star Formation

Under the supervision of Peter Martin, I completed my undergraduate thesis on the effects of Herschel's angular resolution degradation on compact sources in regions of massive star formation. Compact sources in giant molecular clouds, which may form an entire cluster of stars, are often extended, superimposed on a fluctuating background, and often crowded. The result is that Herschel measures increasingly larger masses as its beam size increases from 70 to 500 micrometers. Additionally, the Galactic cirrus, which can introduce contamination, is further complicated by the high degree of clustering typical in these environments. For this project, I used high-resolution JCMT images of W3 to quantify these effects. Since JCMT's 450 micron passband is similiar in wavelength to Herschel's 500 micron passband, it can be used to directly measure this problem of contamination.
